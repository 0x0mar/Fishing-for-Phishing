    <div class="post-content"><p>Showcasing products in photos can have many forms. Some opt for simplicity, but many go the route of creating context in their photos and it really pays off when props and styling are beautifully executed. These spring inspired images from <a href="https://cottonideastudio.com/index.htm" target="_blank">Cotton Idea Studio</a> are perfectly arranged and make the products even more enticing.</p>
<p><img src="http://evie-s.com/news/wp-content/uploads/2008/04/cottonidea.jpg" alt="Product Photos" /></p>
<p>Companies like <a href="http://www.anthropologie.com">Anthropologie</a> and <a href="http://www.nantakajoy.com" target="_blank">Nantaka Joy</a> do this type of thing very well. What are some of your favorite product photos to look at and be inspired by?</p>

		<div id="post-222" class="post-222 post type-post status-publish format-standard hentry category-art category-diy category-things-of-beauty tag-seashell-craft tag-seashell-diy-project tag-st-johns">
    <h2 class="post-title"><a href="http://evie-s.com/news/2008/04/11/what-to-do-with-broken-seashells/" rel="bookmark" title="Permalink to What to Do With Broken Seashells">What to Do With Broken Seashells</a></h2>

    <div class="post-meta">
	    <span class="post-author">
		    by		    <a href="http://evie-s.com/news/author/evies./" title="Posts by evie s." rel="author">evie s.</a>	    </span><!-- .post-author -->

	    <span class="post-date">
		on		April 11, 2008,
	    </span><!-- .post-author -->

	    <span class="post-comments">
		<a href="http://evie-s.com/news/2008/04/11/what-to-do-with-broken-seashells/#comments">0 comments</a>
	    </span><!-- .post-author -->
    </div><!-- END .post-meta -->

    	
    
    <div class="post-content"><p>Over Thanksgiving one year my family took a vacation to <a href="http://www.usvi.net/usvi/stj.html" target="_blank">St. John&#8217;s</a> in the U.S. Virgin Islands. It was beautiful and memorable to say the least. My husband and I spent every spare moment scouring <a href="http://www.donaldschnell.com/" target="_blank">our private beach</a> for seashells and other treasures. After days of this, we accumulated quite a pile of beautiful shells, some perfectly intact, and lots of broken pieces. While we traveled light on this trip, I think we added about 20-30 lbs to our luggage on our return trip.</p>
<p>Once we were home I looked at all we had collected and thought, &#8220;what now?&#8221; In my quest to find a seashell DIY project, I found one really good one. So I decided to try out these <a href="http://www.hgtv.com/hgtv/crafting/article/0,1789,HGTV_3352_1812783,00.html" target="_blank">Glowing Beach Votives</a>, and ended up making one for myself and all the girls in my family.</p>
<p><img src="http://evie-s.com/news/wp-content/uploads/2008/04/votives.jpg" alt="Glowing Seashell Votive" /></p>
<p>I love them! It was the perfect way to display all those beautiful broken pieces that I thought would be useless except to pack away and look at on occasion. Here are the instructions.</p>
<p>Materials:</p>
<p>FIMO Soft translucent clay<br />
white or natural colored sand<br />
mini shells, broken shell pieces<br />
round glass votive<br />
craft knife<br />
pasta machine or clay roller<br />
votive candle</p>
<p>Steps:</p>
<ol>
<li>Condition clay (with pasta machine or by hand rolling). Make a sheet of the FIMO roughly as thick as cereal-box cardboard (setting 5 on the pasta machine).</li>
<li>Cut one end of the sheet of FIMO with the craft knife to give a straight cut and lay this onto the glass votive. Stretch and press the clay to lie flat on the glass surface. Cut where it overlaps and press to join. Using a smooth, nonporous item (such as a glass), roll over the clay in different directions to remove any air pockets.</li>
<li>With the craft knife, trim the extra FIMO from the bottom and top of the votive. Smooth the edges with your fingers to round off.</li>
<li>Use more FIMO to make another flat sheet that is the thickest setting of your pasta machine. Trim one side of the sheet with the craft knife for a straight edge. Trim the other side in a wavy line.</li>
<li>Align the straight edge of this clay sheet with the top of the votive and lay the wavy sheet over the first layer. Trim where the sheet overlaps and press to remove any seam. (You may need to trim the wavy piece to join together.) Press this layer of the FIMO onto the previous covering to remove any bumps or air bubbles.</li>
<li>Begin embedding small pieces of sea glass and shells into the top, thicker area of the FIMO. Press the polymer around the edges of the shells and glass to secure them.</li>
<li>Pour sand in a pan or plastic bag. Roll the votive in the sand, pressing the sand into the FIMO to secure.</li>
<li>Bake per the clay manufacturer&#8217;s guidelines. Allow to cool. Add a candle and enjoy your glowing beach votive!</li>
</ol>
