    <h2 class="post-title"><a href="http://evie-s.com/news/2013/12/04/florida-treasure/" rel="bookmark" title="Permalink to Florida Treasure">Florida Treasure</a></h2>

    <div class="post-meta">
	    <span class="post-author">
		    by		    <a href="http://evie-s.com/news/author/evies./" title="Posts by evie s." rel="author">evie s.</a>	    </span><!-- .post-author -->

	    <span class="post-date">
		on		December 4, 2013,
	    </span><!-- .post-author -->

	    <span class="post-comments">
		<a href="http://evie-s.com/news/2013/12/04/florida-treasure/#comments">1 comment</a>
	    </span><!-- .post-author -->

    <div class="post-content"><p>Florida was amazing. We were there for 3 weeks while Kent worked closely with <a href="http://onehope.net/">OneHope</a> on some online intiatives. The boys and I were at the beach often and we came home with some mighty good tan lines. The treasures were abundant on land, shore and in the water. So this collection was hard to put together, just because there was so much bounty.</p>
<p><a href="http://evie-s.com/news/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/Florida2.jpg" rel="image_group"><img class="alignnone size-full wp-image-3655" alt="Florida2" src="http://evie-s.com/news/wp-content/uploads/2013/12/Florida2.jpg" width="602" height="753" /></a></p>
<blockquote><p>The sea can do craziness, it can do smooth, It can lie down like silk breathing or toss havoc shoreward; it can give gifts or withhold all; it can rise, ebb, froth like an incoming frenzy of fountains, or it can sweet-talk entirely.     <div class="post-content"><p>We are 23 days into our RV adventure. There have been highs and lows. The first week I was convinced that we wouldn&#8217;t make it. Living a life of terrific ease and comfort all the time never really taught anyone anything (that I know of). Here are the top 10 observations from a newbie traveler:</p>
<h4>DOWNSIDES</h4>
<p>1. Tight space = loss of dignity and personal space</p>
<p>2. Less time to work (for me)</p>
<p>3. No consistency</p>
<p>4. Nowhere to put a screaming child</p>
<p>5. Carsickness :( Anyone have any tips on remedies?</p>
<p>6. One wrong switch flipped in the RV while driving into a gas station, we all blow up. For realz.</p>
<p>7. Um, I ran out of &#8220;downsides&#8221; :)</p>
<h4>UPSIDES</h4>
<p>1. Meeting new people</p>
<p>2. Seeing new places</p>
<p>3. Building love and togetherness as a family</p>
<p>4. A Simpler life</p>
<p>6. Clean the whole house in an hour or less!</p>
<p>7. Flexibility</p>
<p>8. Focus on priorities in life &#8211; relationships, food, water, shelter, work &#8211; that pretty much sums it up</p>
<p>9. More time to read, write and journal!</p>
<p>10. A stronger, more intense season of walking with the Lord, listening and abiding</p>

<p>Last week was <a href="http://www.circlesconference.com">Circles Conference 2013</a>, and just like last year, I left feeling charged, full, and energized for the creative journey ahead. My favorite talk, and most applicable right now, was Ben Jenkins from <a href="http://www.onefastbuffalo.com">One Fast Buffalo</a>. Six years ago he traded his overdone life for the bare essentials life of living in an RV. Kent and I got talk to him at the <a href="https://www.facebook.com/circlesconference">speakers dinner</a> and pick his brain. The main takeaway from our conversation, was, &#8220;it&#8217;s all about togetherness.&#8221; We are 12 days into our journey on the road and that is for sure what I&#8217;m seeing. But, more about that later.</p>

<p><a href="http://evie-s.com/news/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/essentials1.gif" rel="image_group"><img class="alignnone size-full wp-image-3605" alt="essentials" src="http://evie-s.com/news/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/essentials1.gif" width="602" height="600" /></a></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>The way he sees it there are 24 hours in a day &#8211; 8 hours for work &#8211; 8 for play &#8211; and 8 for sleeping. His best work is done in solitude and he believes that every artist needs solitude  plus participation (exploring, interacting with people, etc) for the best work to come. There definitely was a common theme through a lot of the talks about creating more with love and integrity and simplifying so you can experience more and focus on relationships. <a href="http://www.lottanieminen.com/">Lotta Nieminen</a> talked about this when she realized she felt more like a factory than a designer, and she was just crossing projects off the list, not doing them with love.</p>
<blockquote><p>Too tired and too busy is very American. Slow down. Slow is better. -Ben Jenkins</p></blockquote>
<p>I also got to meet a designer I admire very much, <a href="http://www.thefreshexchangeblog.com/">Megan Gilger</a>. Her talk was wonderful and her slides were just delicious. I love everything she creates, right down to the life that her and her husband lives.</p>
<p><a href="http://evie-s.com/news/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/circles2.jpg" rel="image_group"><img class="alignnone size-full wp-image-3603" alt="circles2" src="http://evie-s.com/news/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/circles2.jpg" width="602" height="602" /></a></p>
<h6>Photo from my <a href="http://instagram.com/evieshaffer">Instagram feed</a></h6>
<p>It was so fun to be with other creatives, but not just creatives who are ambitious and talented, but Circles attracts a very special crowd that seems to want more from creativity than just big name clients, money or building a big name. It might sound cliche, but there is definitely a commonality in wanting to use art and creativity for good and to bring change in the world. That is what I love about this conference. I walked away with a lot to process and a lot to build upon.</p>

    <h2 class="post-title"><a href="http://evie-s.com/news/2013/08/21/art-practicality/" rel="bookmark" title="Permalink to Art &amp; Practicality">Art &#038; Practicality</a></h2>

    <div class="post-meta">
	    <span class="post-author">
		    by		    <a href="http://evie-s.com/news/author/evies./" title="Posts by evie s." rel="author">evie s.</a>	    </span><!-- .post-author -->

	    <span class="post-date">
		on		August 21, 2013,
	    </span><!-- .post-author -->

	    <span class="post-comments">
		<a href="http://evie-s.com/news/2013/08/21/art-practicality/#comments">4 comments</a>
	    </span><!-- .post-author -->
    </div><!-- END .post-meta -->

    	
    
    <div class="post-content"><p>I recently finished up a 6-week acrylic painting class with instructor and painter, <a href="http://fineartamerica.com/profiles/bert-seabourn.html">Bert Seabourn</a>. I’ve loved his work since being introduced to it about a year ago, and my husband thought it was high time I take his class at City Arts in OKC. Bert is awesome. A color and composition genius. He works really hard at his craft – working at least 8 hours a day. He’s 82 years old now and he will tell you the best thing about this point in his career, is that 9 out of 10 paintings he creates are good. His skill is so consistent, that you can bet on a masterpiece every time he’s put down the last brush stroke of paint.</p>
<p>Awhile ago I was reading an interview with a very well-known and talented artist whose work I really admire. I won&#8217;t quote his name out of respect for him, but something he said really bugged me. The question was, “What does art do for the world?” His answer was puzzling. He said, “I don’t believe that art does anything for the world, just consume resources like every other human activity.” What??! I wholeheartedly disagree. Art can transcend cultural barriers. Art can move and inspire. Art can convey truth that sometimes words alone fail to do. Art is just as much a journey of discovery for the artist as it is for the viewer. How can that be in vain?</p>
<p>While I&#8217;m sure many can argue the practicality of art, for me personally it is an essential tool for discovering the world and the Creator. Without art, I would shrivel up, the world would seem dull and lonely. Without encountering and engaging beauty, I&#8217;m not sure that my faith would be so strong. This is why I&#8217;m seeking to improve my skills &#8211; so maybe I can communicate the holy creativity of God that much better. I may not be able to put in the hours that Bert does, but I&#8217;m certainly going to be disciplined to put in more than I have in the past.</p>
<p>Bert is someone that will contend for art and the practicality of it. Through his example, my love for art has grown even more. After ending the course, in between meeting so many new friends while talking and drinking wine and sangria, there was definitely a confirmation in the discovery that I love painting portraits. I have always been drawn to portraits. Actually, painting anything else feels like mundane work, but painting people – there is something so intimate, raw, familiar and spiritual about painting a human that keeps me coming back to it again and again.</p>
<p><a href="http://evie-s.com/news/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/lady1.jpg" rel="image_group"><img class="alignnone size-full wp-image-3564" alt="lady1" src="http://evie-s.com/news/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/lady1.jpg" width="602" height="452" /></a></p>
<blockquote><p>An interest in the subject; something you want to say definitely about the subject; this is the first condition of a portrait. Completion does not depend on material representation. The work is done when that special thing has been said.<br />
-Robert Henri, The Art Spirit</p></blockquote>
<p><a href="http://evie-s.com/news/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/022.jpg" rel="image_group"><img class="alignnone size-full wp-image-3567" alt="022" src="http://evie-s.com/news/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/022.jpg" width="602" height="452" /></a></p>
<p>Anyway, these are a couple of mine that came out of the class. I hope to be painting while on the road this fall. I’m sure I will encounter plenty of beautiful subjects while on our travels. My hope is that our lives and stories will intertwine with many along the way, and much &#8220;impractical&#8221; art will come out of it.</p>
</div>
